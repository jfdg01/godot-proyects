Good context — I can see exactly where the confusion is coming from. Let me explain the
  Godot-first way of thinking about this, and why it'll feel cleaner than what you've got.

  The core idea: a pipe pair is one scene, not two pipes glued together in main

  Right now main.gd is doing a lot of reaching into the pipe
  (pipe.get_node("Area2D/CollisionShape2D"), nine_patch.size.y = screen_h, flipping scale.y
  to fake the top pipe). That's the part fighting you. In Godot the guiding principle is:

  ▎ Each scene owns its own layout and behavior. Outsiders talk to it through a small,
  ▎ clean interface — they don't rearrange its insides.

  So the question isn't "how do I spawn two pipes and position them" — it's "what is the
  thing I'm spawning?" In Flappy Bird the thing is a pipe pair: a top pipe, a bottom pipe,
  and a gap between them, all moving left together as a unit. That should be a single
  scene.

  Recommended structure (composition)

  You keep your existing pipe.tscn as the single pipe (the visual + collision). Then you
  build a new scene on top of it:

  PipePair (Node2D)            ← script lives here, moves left, handles cleanup
  ├── TopPipe (instance of pipe.tscn)     ← flipped to point down
  ├── BottomPipe (instance of pipe.tscn)  ← normal, points up
  ├── ScoreArea (Area2D)        ← optional: the gap, for counting points
  └── VisibleOnScreenNotifier2D ← tells the pair when it's left the screen

  The trick that makes the math disappear: position the children once, in the editor,
  relative to the pair's origin. Treat the PipePair's origin as the center of the gap.

  - The bottom pipe sits a little below origin (its top edge at +gap/2), extending down
  off-screen.
  - The top pipe sits a little above origin (its bottom edge at -gap/2), extending up
  off-screen, visually flipped.

  Once that's set up in the editor, you never recompute per-pipe positions in code again.

  What moves to where (the responsibility shift)

  A few Godot-first details worth knowing

  1. Use @export to expose a clean interface. If you want a configurable gap, put @export 
  var gap_size: float on PipePair.gd and let it position its two children in _ready(). Now
  the gap is a knob you can see and tweak in the Inspector — that's very Godot. The outside
  world sets intent (gap_size, position.y), never raw child geometry.

  2. Spawn from a Timer, not from _physics_process + input. Your current spawn-on-keypress
  is a fine debugging tool, but the real game uses a Timer node with wait_time set,
  autostart on, and its timeout signal connected to your spawn function. Timers are nodes
  you configure in the editor — reach for them instead of counting delta yourself.

  3. Clean up off-screen pipes. Add a VisibleOnScreenNotifier2D to the pair and connect its
  screen_exited signal to queue_free(). Otherwise every pipe you've ever spawned keeps
  living and moving forever, off to the left.

  4. Don't flip with negative scale. Negative scale.y works visually but it silently
  inverts collision shapes and child coordinate systems, which causes confusing bugs later.
  For the top pipe prefer flip_v on a Sprite, or rotation = PI (180°) on that pipe
  instance, or just author a NinePatchRect that grows upward. Set it once in the editor on
  TopPipe.

  5. Scoring is its own Area2D in the gap. The empty space between the pipes gets an Area2D
  (your ScoreArea). When the bird's body enters it, +1 point. This is why making the pair
  a real scene pays off — the gap becomes a thing you can attach behavior to.

  The mental model to take away

  You had it inside-out: main was the brain micromanaging dumb pipes. Flip it — make the 
  pipe pair a smart, self-contained scene that knows how to lay itself out, move, score,
  and clean up. Then main becomes a dumb spawner that just drops pairs at the right edge at
  a random height on a timer.
